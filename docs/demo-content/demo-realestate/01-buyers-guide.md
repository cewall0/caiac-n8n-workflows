# Buyer's Guide — Hargrove Realty Group

## Getting Pre-Approved

Before you start touring homes, get pre-approved for a mortgage — not just pre-qualified. Pre-approval means a lender has reviewed your income, assets, and credit and issued a written commitment for a specific loan amount. Sellers take pre-approved buyers far more seriously, and in a competitive market it can be the difference between getting a home and losing it.

You'll need to provide:
- Two most recent pay stubs
- Two years of W-2s or tax returns (self-employed buyers will need additional documentation)
- Two to three months of bank statements
- Government-issued ID
- Authorization for the lender to pull your credit

Pre-approval letters are typically valid for 60–90 days. If your search runs longer, you may need to refresh it.

## How We Work With Buyers

We'll help you identify homes that match your criteria, schedule tours, write competitive offers, negotiate on your behalf, and guide you through every step from contract to close.

## Making an Offer

When you find the right home, we'll pull comparable sales to determine a competitive offer price. Your offer will include:

- **Purchase price** — what you're offering to pay
- **Earnest money deposit** — typically 1–3% of the purchase price, paid within 2–3 days of accepted offer; shows good faith and applies toward your down payment at closing
- **Contingencies** — standard protections including financing, inspection, and appraisal
- **Closing date** — typically 30–45 days from accepted offer
- **Inclusions/exclusions** — specific items you want to be included (appliances, fixtures, etc.)

Sellers typically respond within 24–48 hours. They may accept, reject, or counter your offer. We'll advise you on how to respond at every stage.

## The Home Inspection

Once your offer is accepted, schedule a home inspection within the inspection contingency window — usually 7–10 days. A licensed inspector will examine the home's structure, systems, and major components. Plan on 2–3 hours and attend if you can.

After the inspection, you'll receive a detailed report. You can:
- Accept the home as-is
- Request repairs before closing
- Ask for a price reduction or closing cost credit
- Walk away (within the contingency window) with your earnest money returned

Not every issue warrants a repair request. We'll help you distinguish between major defects and normal wear so you're negotiating effectively, not chasing minor items.

## From Contract to Closing

After the inspection period, your lender will order an appraisal — the bank's independent assessment of the home's value. If the home appraises at or above your purchase price, you proceed. If it appraises below, you can renegotiate the price, pay the difference in cash, or exit under the appraisal contingency.

Your lender will also complete a full underwriting review. Expect requests for additional documentation — this is normal. Respond quickly to keep things on track.

Closing day: you'll sign documents (typically 30–60 minutes), pay closing costs and your down payment, and receive the keys. Closing costs generally run 2–5% of the loan amount and include lender fees, title insurance, prepaid taxes and insurance, and recording fees. We'll give you a detailed estimate well in advance.
