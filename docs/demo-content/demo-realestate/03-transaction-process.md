# The Transaction Process — Hargrove Realty Group

## From Accepted Offer to Closing: What to Expect

A typical real estate transaction takes 30–45 days from accepted offer to closing. Here's what happens at each stage.

## Days 1–3: Earnest Money and Title

**Earnest money deposit** — The buyer delivers earnest money (typically 1–3% of purchase price) to the title company within 2–3 business days of the accepted offer. This deposit is held in escrow and applied toward the buyer's down payment and closing costs at closing. If the buyer backs out for a covered contingency reason, the money is returned. If the buyer backs out without a valid contingency, the seller typically keeps the deposit.

**Title search begins** — The title company starts researching the property's ownership history to ensure the seller has clear, marketable title. They're looking for unpaid liens, judgments, boundary disputes, or other encumbrances that would complicate the transfer.

## Days 3–10: Home Inspection

The buyer hires a licensed home inspector to evaluate the property's condition. The inspection takes 2–3 hours. The buyer will receive a detailed written report.

After reviewing the report, the buyer may:
- Proceed as-is
- Request specific repairs
- Request a price reduction or closing cost credit in lieu of repairs
- Terminate the contract within the contingency window (earnest money returned)

Both parties negotiate any repair requests. Once resolved, the inspection contingency is removed in writing.

## Days 7–21: Appraisal

The buyer's lender orders an appraisal — an independent assessment of the property's market value by a licensed appraiser. The lender requires this to ensure they're not lending more than the home is worth.

- If the home appraises **at or above** the purchase price: the transaction proceeds normally.
- If the home appraises **below** the purchase price: the parties must negotiate — price reduction, buyer pays the difference in cash, or the contract may be terminated under the appraisal contingency.

Appraisals typically take 7–14 days to complete and report.

## Days 10–30: Underwriting

While the appraisal is in progress, the buyer's lender is working through full underwriting — verifying all financial documentation. Expect requests for updated pay stubs, bank statements, or explanations of unusual deposits. Respond to lender requests within 24 hours to avoid delays.

The lender will issue a "Clear to Close" (CTC) once underwriting is complete. This is the green light that the loan is approved.

## Days 25–35: Title Clearance and Closing Disclosure

The title company resolves any title issues found during the search and issues a title commitment. Title insurance (both lender's and owner's policies) is ordered at this stage.

The buyer's lender issues a Closing Disclosure (CD) at least 3 business days before closing. This document details all final loan terms and closing costs. Review it carefully and contact your lender immediately with any questions.

## Closing Day

Closing takes place at the title company's office. Sellers and buyers may close at the same time or separately.

**Buyers bring:**
- Government-issued photo ID
- Cashier's check or confirmed wire transfer for closing funds (down payment + closing costs, minus any credits)

**Sellers bring:**
- Government-issued photo ID
- Keys, garage door openers, and any access codes
- Any agreed-upon items not physically present at the property

The title officer walks through the documents, both parties sign, funds are transferred, and the deed is recorded with the county. At recording, ownership officially transfers. Keys are handed over.

Sellers typically receive their net proceeds by wire transfer the same day or the following business day.

## Common Causes of Delay

- Buyer's financing issues (new debt, job change, documentation gaps)
- Appraisal coming in below purchase price
- Title issues (unpaid liens, estate matters, boundary disputes)
- Repair negotiations running long
- Survey issues
- HOA document delays (for properties in HOAs)

We track every milestone and stay in close contact with all parties to catch problems early. Most delays can be managed with good communication and quick action.
