# Greenfield CPA — Common Tax Questions

## Deductions

**Can I deduct my home office?**
Yes, if you use a dedicated space in your home regularly and exclusively for business. The space must be your principal place of business or where you meet clients.

- **Simplified method:** $5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft (max $1,500 deduction). Easy to calculate but may leave money on the table.
- **Actual expense method:** Deduct the business-use percentage of actual home expenses — mortgage interest/rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation. More paperwork but often a larger deduction.

**Important:** W-2 employees who work from home (even full-time remote workers) cannot deduct home office expenses on their federal return since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act eliminated this deduction for employees. This deduction is only available to the self-employed.

**Can I deduct my vehicle?**
Yes, for the business-use portion. You cannot deduct commuting miles (home to your regular place of business). Business driving includes client visits, business errands, travel to a second location, etc.

Two methods:
- **Standard mileage rate:** 67 cents per business mile for 2024. Multiply your business miles by this rate. Requires a mileage log (date, destination, business purpose, miles).
- **Actual expense method:** Deduct the business-use percentage of actual vehicle costs (gas, insurance, maintenance, depreciation). More complex but may be better for high-cost vehicles or very high business use.

**Can I deduct meals?**
Business meals are 50% deductible when there is a legitimate business discussion before, during, or after the meal, and you document: who you met with, the business purpose, date, and amount. Entertainment expenses (sporting events, concerts) are no longer deductible under current law.

**What about the home office and vehicle — do I need to report them if I sell my home or car?**
Yes — if you've claimed depreciation on your home office, a portion of your home sale gain may be taxable. Vehicle depreciation claimed must be "recaptured" as ordinary income when the vehicle is sold or disposed of. We track this for all depreciation clients.

## Self-Employment

**Do I have to pay quarterly estimated taxes?**
If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax after withholding, you generally must make quarterly estimated payments to avoid an underpayment penalty. Quarterly due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15.

For 2024, you avoid penalties by paying the lesser of:
- 100% of your 2023 tax liability (110% if 2023 AGI was over $150,000)
- 90% of your 2024 tax liability

We calculate your recommended quarterly payments at the start of the year and update them mid-year if your income changes significantly.

**What is self-employment tax?**
Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer share of Social Security and Medicare taxes (combined 15.3% on net self-employment income up to $168,600; 2.9% Medicare on amounts above). You can deduct half of this tax on your return, which slightly reduces your income tax.

**What retirement accounts can I use to reduce self-employment income?**
Several options with generous contribution limits:
- **SEP-IRA:** Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $69,000 for 2024. Simple to set up; contributions can be made up to tax filing deadline including extensions.
- **Solo 401(k):** Higher effective contribution limit for high earners. Employee deferral ($23,000 for 2024 + $7,500 catch-up if over 50) plus employer contribution (25% of compensation). Must be established by December 31.
- **SIMPLE IRA:** Good for businesses with employees; simpler rules than a full 401(k).

## Life Events

**I got married this year — what do I need to do?**
Notify your employer to update your W-4 withholding. Determine whether to file jointly or separately (nearly always jointly is better, but we'll model both). Update your name with the SSA if you changed it — name/SSN mismatches cause IRS processing problems.

**I had a baby — what tax benefits apply?**
- Child Tax Credit: $2,000 per qualifying child under 17 (partially refundable)
- Child and Dependent Care Credit: If you paid for daycare so you could work
- Dependent care FSA: Up to $5,000 pre-tax through employer benefit plans
- Earned Income Tax Credit: May apply depending on your income level

**I sold my house — do I owe taxes?**
If the home was your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years, you can exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 if married filing jointly) from taxation. Gain above the exclusion is taxable. Keep records of your purchase price, closing costs, and improvements — these reduce your taxable gain.

**I started a business — what can I deduct in year one?**
Startup costs up to $5,000 can be deducted in the year the business opens (amounts above $5,000 are amortized over 15 years). Equipment purchases may qualify for Section 179 expensing (up to $1.16 million in 2024) or bonus depreciation (60% in 2024, phasing down). Come see us before year-end if you have significant equipment purchases planned.

## IRS Notices

**I received a letter from the IRS — what should I do?**
Don't ignore it. Bring it to us. Most IRS notices are routine (CP2000 notices proposing changes, balance due notices, requests for documentation) and can be resolved without penalty if responded to promptly.

**Do not call the number on the notice** without consulting us first — we can handle the response on your behalf.

Do call us immediately for: audit notices (CP75, CP75A, Letter 2205), collection notices (CP503, CP504), and any notice with a response deadline of 30 days or less.
