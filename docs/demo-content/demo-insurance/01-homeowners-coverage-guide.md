# Mountain State Insurance — Homeowners Coverage Guide

## What Is Homeowners Insurance?

A homeowners insurance policy is a package policy that combines property coverage (protecting your home and belongings) with liability coverage (protecting you if someone is injured on your property or you cause damage to others). Most mortgage lenders require it; even if you own your home outright, it protects your largest asset.

## What's Covered

### Dwelling Coverage (Coverage A)
Covers the physical structure of your home — walls, roof, floors, built-in appliances, and attached structures like a garage. Pays to repair or rebuild if your home is damaged by a covered peril.

**Covered perils** (standard HO-3 policy):
- Fire and smoke
- Lightning
- Windstorm and hail
- Explosions
- Vandalism and malicious mischief
- Theft
- Accidental water damage from burst pipes or appliance overflow
- Falling objects (tree branch, aircraft)
- Weight of ice, snow, or sleet

**Coverage amount:** Your dwelling should be insured for its replacement cost — what it would cost to rebuild with similar materials today, not the market value of the home. Our agents calculate this using a replacement cost estimator.

### Other Structures (Coverage B)
Covers detached structures on your property: detached garage, fence, shed, swimming pool, gazebo. Standard coverage is 10% of your dwelling limit (e.g., $30,000 if dwelling is insured for $300,000). Can be increased.

### Personal Property (Coverage C)
Covers your belongings — furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances — against covered perils. Standard coverage is 50–70% of your dwelling limit.

**Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value:** We offer both. Replacement cost pays what it costs to buy new. Actual cash value deducts depreciation (a 5-year-old TV is not worth what a new one costs). Replacement cost coverage is more expensive but far better for most homeowners.

**High-value items:** Jewelry, art, collectibles, musical instruments, and firearms may have sub-limits under standard personal property coverage (e.g., jewelry is typically capped at $1,500–$2,500 for theft). Schedule these items separately for full value — see Policy Endorsements.

### Loss of Use (Coverage D)
If your home is uninhabitable due to a covered loss, this pays for temporary housing (hotel, rental) and additional living expenses (restaurant meals above your normal food budget). Typically 20–30% of your dwelling coverage limit.

### Liability (Coverage E)
Covers you if a guest is injured on your property, your dog bites someone, or you accidentally cause damage to a neighbor's property. Pays for legal defense and damages up to your policy limit. Standard policies include $100,000; we recommend $300,000–$500,000 for most homeowners. An umbrella policy can add $1–$5 million on top.

### Medical Payments (Coverage F)
Pays medical bills for guests injured on your property, regardless of fault — up to $1,000–$5,000 per person. Does not apply to you or household members.

## What Is NOT Covered

Standard homeowners policies do NOT cover:

- **Flooding** — water that enters from outside (rain, overflowing rivers, storm surge). Requires a separate flood insurance policy through FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or private flood coverage. Mountain State Insurance can help you obtain flood coverage.
- **Earthquakes** — requires a separate earthquake endorsement or policy
- **Sewer/sump pump backup** — not covered by default; available as an endorsement ($50–$100/year) — highly recommended in Indiana
- **Normal wear and tear and maintenance issues** — a leaky roof that developed over time, a furnace that died of old age, foundation settling
- **Intentional damage** — self-explanatory
- **Business activities** — running a business from home may not be covered; ask about home business endorsements

## How to Read Your Declarations Page

Your declarations page (the first page of your policy packet) summarizes your coverage, limits, and deductibles. Key items to check:
- Dwelling coverage limit (Coverage A) — should be replacement cost, not purchase price
- Deductible for standard claims
- Wind/hail deductible (often separate and higher, expressed as a % of dwelling coverage)
- Names and address on the policy
